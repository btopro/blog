The wheels squeaked on the cart, jerking it away from the rest of them to get out to put my kid sister in. The exact age I'm unaware, but I remember being fourteen or fifteen. Walking into the grocery store in Scranton, PA, it was like any other day of my youth.

Oldest of three, my youngest sister is ten years (almost to the day) younger than me. In millennial terms we are effectively different generations.

There, pushing her in the cart that day, she used a phrase that I'll never forget; as evidenced by such a specific memory around a phrase.

As I pushed my sister through the produce isle me and her were being silly, the way we always were growing up. My mom walked away to get something and a mother and daughter walked up and commented politely..

Is that your Daughter? She's adorable

Before I could say no, my sister started yelling "Daddy, Daddy!". I remember giving her a look and we went on about our day, telling my parents who also thought she was being funny. Even at 5, trolling me.

I remember that moment as the first time I knew I wanted to be a dad. That title , that responsibility , was from that moment on that it held so much weight to me. It seemed a bit heavy, even for that time, to want that responsibility.

I mean, I was the oldest in my family and oldest grandchild, only boy on both sides for a very long time, I was used to helping corral everyone or not having anyone into the same things as me.

Even before my first was born I remember saying to myself I can fail at anything, I can lose it all and be terrible at my job or anything... but not being a parent. Being their dad is, and always has been, something I've taken with the utmost seriousness. They are what I've wanted, these moments I get to live in, are those I sought all the way back then.

I remember how close we were growing up with that gap, that caretaker, trolling about being Dad relationship. I remember dating my "high school sweetheart" (we still say hi once a year and play catch up) and how my little sister got attached to her.

Children imprint on many people around that age (this would have been six or seven). I remember her asking if she could come over to play, or if she could take her to the park. It was like she gained a friend too. I remember us discussing at the time how important this relationship was... referencing her and my sister.

It was around this time I really got into Everclear (the band) and they were on the radio all the time. I got their CD called Learning How to Smile and just eating the songs up. But one in particular, would just slice through me.


It's called Annabella's Song. It's why my wife says she's glad we don't have girls. Because of how I get around babies, how I fall back into that "Daddy" in the grocery store mode. Well, it's because of this song.

I remember going on walks around my neighborhood when I needed to think late at night in college (home on break or summer). I didn't have a great college experience.. but it was really evidenced in how crushed I was to not get to see my little kid, my kid sister when I went off to school.

I cried, A lot when I went to school. It was hard to move from a bubble to the "real world" (which is also a massive bubble, wow was I sheltered..).

Anyway, I remember staring up at the moon, heart broken at the time because of a failed relationship.. and playing this song as loud as I could and just crying the whole walk. I couldn't stop listening to it. The idea that I was away from my kid, that I was growing up, and that she would be hurt (which she wasn't) by my girlfriend and I breaking up...

I see now how insane it all sounds. The rationalizing of a child about what's important, what will impact others..

I only pieced these things back together because that initial phrase came up from a little girl.. a rambunctious, awesome, adorable, funny three year old who just "To hold you up above the world".

Who knew. With all my walls, my locks and doors, my sarcasm (crutch), and abrasiveness... that I could be defeated by a three year old.

I love playing with kids and they seem to enjoy me. I usually have stupid hats on, or jerseys, or being really tall and generally goofy looking or something that's got to make me stand out as a "mark" for paying more attention to kids than adults. They give us life, they are the next generation... they are everything.

Anyway, the few times she's seen me (best friend's daughter) she asks me to help her touch the ceiling. After all, her house has tall ceilings and I'm a glorified step ladder wearing a Disney hat (no really) for conference wear. So... obviously.

I always stick her on the ceiling or a wall or whatever; more or less till I'm the one tired cause three year olds have boundless energy from breathing and infectious laughs that power them until they crash.

Again, I haven't seen her that often, but she asks when I'm coming and is excited by the idea of having someone that can stick her on a ceiling (though I was allowed to check out the princess fort last time, poor insulation and I have to lay on the floor in order to look in but I'd give it a good yelp score).

But as this is unpacking, and if the last week is any indicator, I'll be unpacking a lot,.. these have to relate some how. We were playing (more like I'm running away and she's chasing me) and she gets too excited and says "Daddy pick me up daddy!"

Broken. Puddle of emotions come flooding into my head. As well as a grimice.. cause beyond me not being Daddy... Daddy's not in the picture. Her mom freezes, I freeze.. I say "So who are you then".

"The big sister"

"That's my little sister" she says, pointing to her mom.

Tension fades.. but I knew as I related this story to my wife that I'd barely be able to tell it.. and that I could barely write it down now.

Because I've taken being a father as my ultimate prize since I was 15 apparently... and everyone deserves that rock, those big shoulders to sit on, to cry on, and to stick up on the ceiling.

I know part of her interest in me is probably that on some level, seeing friends, I match that imprint of a strong male figure; a role, however how small, however infrequent an occurrence, that I can play; that I am honored to be able to play.

That same imprint I worried my sister did with my girlfriend all those years ago. And as I know there is now, that same worry associated with that imprinting. But she turned out great (more than great), and I know this one will too. She's too strong and her mom's too dedicated to her success for her not to turn out awesome.

I'm still that 15 year old proud to be called Dad accidentally. And I'm a sappy, happy, emotional wreck, anytime I can go back to playing that part. Cause even acting that part for a few moments is validating.

After all, through all the work, the hours, the tears.. I only have one real goal at the end of this life: to be a great dad.
